The Consumer‚Äôs Problem

Starting Point: What Can You Afford? üí∏

Every consumer faces three constraints:

üí∞ Income (\(M\))

The total money available to spend

üè∑Ô∏è Price of Good 1 (\(p_1\))

How much each unit of good 1 costs

üè∑Ô∏è Price of Good 2 (\(p_2\))

How much each unit of good 2 costs

The Consumer‚Äôs Problem

Given income \(M\) and prices \(p_1\), \(p_2\), what combinations of goods 1 and 2 can the consumer purchase?

A Tourism Example üå¥

Scenario: A tourist arrives in Lisbon with a daily budget of ‚Ç¨100.

Two ‚Äúgoods‚Äù to spend on:

  • üç¥ Meals at restaurants: ‚Ç¨20 each
  • üé´ Museum tickets: ‚Ç¨10 each

Question: What combinations of meals and museum visits can this tourist afford?

Meals (\(x_1\)) Museums (\(x_2\)) Total Spent
0 10 ‚Ç¨100
1 8 ‚Ç¨100
2 6 ‚Ç¨100
3 4 ‚Ç¨100
5 0 ‚Ç¨100

The Budget Constraint Equation üìè

The consumer spends all income on two goods:

\[\underbrace{p_1 \cdot x_1 + p_2 \cdot x_2}_{Expenditure} = \underbrace{M}_{Budget}\]

From our example: ‚Ç¨20 \(\cdot x_1\) + ‚Ç¨10 \(\cdot x_2\) = ‚Ç¨100

Solving for \(x_2\) (to graph it):

\[x_2 = \frac{M}{p_2} - \frac{p_1}{p_2} \cdot x_1\]

\[x_2 = \frac{100}{10} - \frac{20}{10} \cdot x_1 = 10 - 2x_1\]

Graphing the Budget Constraint üìâ

Key Elements of the Budget Constraint

Intercepts (maximum of each good):

  • Vertical (\(x_1 = 0\)): \(\frac{M}{p_2} = \frac{100}{10} = 10\) museums

  • Horizontal (\(x_2 = 0\)): \(\frac{M}{p_1} = \frac{100}{20} = 5\) meals

Slope of the budget line:

\[\text{Slope} = -\frac{p_1}{p_2} = -\frac{20}{10} = -2\]

üëâ For every 1 extra meal, the tourist gives up 2 museum visits

This is the economic rate of substitution set by the market! Also known as the opportunity cost of an extra meal!

Budget Set vs Budget Line üó∫Ô∏è

Budget Line vs Budget Set

  • Budget Line All bundles where the consumer spends exactly all income: \(p_1 x_1 + p_2 x_2 = M\)

  • Budget Set All affordable bundles (spend all or less): \(p_1 x_1 + p_2 x_2 \leq M\)

The budget set is the shaded area including the line itself.

Bundles above the budget line are unaffordable ‚ùå

Bundles on the budget line: spend all income ‚úÖ

Bundles inside the budget set: affordable with money left over ‚úÖ

What Shifts the Budget Constraint?

Change in Income üìà

What happens if our tourist‚Äôs budget increases from ‚Ç¨100 to ‚Ç¨150?

üëâ Income change: parallel shift (slope unchanged at \(-p_1/p_2\))

Change in Price of Good 1 üè∑Ô∏è

What if meal prices drop from ‚Ç¨20 to ‚Ç¨10?

üëâ Price change of one good: pivot around the other intercept (slope changes!)

Summary: What Shifts What? üìù

Change Effect on Budget Line Slope Intercepts
‚¨ÜÔ∏è Income (\(M\)) Parallel shift outward Same Both increase
‚¨áÔ∏è Income (\(M\)) Parallel shift inward Same Both decrease
‚¨áÔ∏è Price \(p_1\) Pivot outward on \(x_1\) axis Flatter \(x_1\)-intercept increases
‚¨ÜÔ∏è Price \(p_1\) Pivot inward on \(x_1\) axis Steeper \(x_1\)-intercept decreases
‚¨áÔ∏è Price \(p_2\) Pivot outward on \(x_2\) axis Steeper \(x_2\)-intercept increases
‚¨ÜÔ∏è Price \(p_2\) Pivot inward on \(x_2\) axis Flatter \(x_2\)-intercept decreases

üí° Key insight: The slope \(-p_1/p_2\) is the relative price ‚Äî what the market says one good costs in terms of the other.

The Slope as Opportunity Cost ‚öñÔ∏è

The slope of the budget constraint has a direct economic interpretation:

\[\text{Slope} = -\frac{p_1}{p_2}\]

This tells us the market‚Äôs exchange rate between the two goods.

Our example: \(-20/10 = -2\)

For 1 extra meal, you must give up 2 museum visits.

This is not a preference ‚Äî it is a constraint imposed by prices!

üëâ Compare with the PPF slope from Lecture 4

  • PPF slope: society‚Äôs opportunity cost (technology)
  • Budget line slope: individual‚Äôs opportunity cost (prices)

Both represent trade-offs, at different scales!

Numerical Example: Step by Step üßÆ

Problem: A tourist has ‚Ç¨200 to spend. Surfing lessons cost ‚Ç¨40 each. Fado show tickets cost ‚Ç¨25 each.

Step 1: Write the budget constraint

\[40 x_1 + 25 x_2 = 200\]

Step 2: Find intercepts

  • If \(x_1 = 0\): \(x_2 = 200/25 = 8\) fado shows
  • If \(x_2 = 0\): \(x_1 = 200/40 = 5\) surf lessons

Step 3: Find the slope

\[\text{Slope} = -\frac{p_1}{p_2} = -\frac{40}{25} = -1.6\]

üëâ 1 extra surf lesson costs 1.6 fado shows

Step 4: Check an interior bundle ‚Äî (2 surf, 4 fado): \(40(2) + 25(4) = 80 + 100 = 180 \leq 200\) ‚úÖ (inside budget set, ‚Ç¨20 unspent)

General Formulas: Cheat Sheet üìã

Budget Constraint Formulas

Equation: \(p_1 x_1 + p_2 x_2 = M\)

Solved for \(x_2\): \(\displaystyle x_2 = \frac{M}{p_2} - \frac{p_1}{p_2} x_1\)

Vertical intercept (\(x_1 = 0\)): \(\displaystyle \frac{M}{p_2}\)

Horizontal intercept (\(x_2 = 0\)): \(\displaystyle \frac{M}{p_1}\)

Slope: \(\displaystyle -\frac{p_1}{p_2}\) (the relative price of good 1 in terms of good 2)

Summary: Today‚Äôs Key Takeaways

Today‚Äôs Lecture Integration:

  1. Income (\(M\)) and prices (\(p_1, p_2\)) define the consumer‚Äôs constraint
  2. Budget line: \(p_1 x_1 + p_2 x_2 = M\) ‚Äî all bundles spending exactly all income
  3. Budget set: \(p_1 x_1 + p_2 x_2 \leq M\) ‚Äî all affordable bundles
  4. Slope = \(-p_1/p_2\) = market rate of exchange between goods
  5. Income changes ‚û°Ô∏è parallel shift
  6. Price changes ‚û°Ô∏è pivot (rotation)

Connection to previous lectures: The budget line is the individual-level analog of the PPF ‚Äî both show feasible combinations and trade-offs.

Exercises

Application Time! ‚úèÔ∏è

Budget constraint calculations and graphical analysis.

Exercise 1: Multiple Choice

Question: A consumer has income \(M = 120\), with \(p_1 = 15\) and \(p_2 = 10\). The slope of the budget constraint is:

A. \(-10/15\)
B. \(-15/10\)
C. \(-120/15\)
D. \(-120/10\)

Answer: B

The slope of the budget line is always \(-p_1/p_2 = -15/10 = -1.5\). This means for each additional unit of good 1, the consumer must give up 1.5 units of good 2. Note: income affects the position of the line, not its slope.

Exercise 2: Multiple Choice

Question: If the price of good 2 doubles while income and \(p_1\) stay the same, the budget line:

A. Shifts outward in parallel
B. Pivots inward around the \(x_1\)-intercept
C. Pivots outward around the \(x_2\)-intercept
D. Pivots inward around the \(x_2\)-intercept

Answer: B

When \(p_2\) doubles: the \(x_2\)-intercept (\(M/p_2\)) halves (moves down), while the \(x_1\)-intercept (\(M/p_1\)) stays the same. So the line pivots inward around the \(x_1\)-intercept. The consumer can buy less of good 2 but the same maximum of good 1. The slope \(-p_1/p_2\) becomes smaller in absolute value (the line becomes flatter).

Exercise 3: Open Question

Scenario: A Portuguese tourism student plans a weekend trip. She has a budget of ‚Ç¨150. She wants to split spending between:

  • üèÑ‚Äç‚ôÄÔ∏è Surf lessons: ‚Ç¨30 each (\(x_1\))
  • üç∑ Wine tasting tours: ‚Ç¨25 each (\(x_2\))

Questions:

  1. Write the budget constraint equation and solve for \(x_2\).

  2. Calculate and interpret the slope. What is the opportunity cost of one surf lesson in terms of wine tastings?

  3. Find both intercepts. Draw the budget constraint with \(x_1\) on the horizontal axis.

  4. Is the bundle (2 surf lessons, 3 wine tastings) affordable? Is it on the budget line?

  5. Suppose the student receives a ‚Ç¨50 gift card (total budget now ‚Ç¨200). Draw the new budget line on the same graph. What changed and what stayed the same?

  6. Now instead of the gift card, suppose surf lesson prices drop to ‚Ç¨25 (budget stays at ‚Ç¨150). Draw this new line. How does it differ from part (e)?

Exercise 3: Solution ‚Äî Parts a, b, c

a) Budget constraint: \(30x_1 + 25x_2 = 150\)

Solving for \(x_2\): \(x_2 = \frac{150}{25} - \frac{30}{25}x_1 = 6 - 1.2x_1\)

b) Slope \(= -p_1/p_2 = -30/25 = -1.2\)

üëâ Each surf lesson costs 1.2 wine tastings. The opportunity cost of 1 surf lesson is 1.2 wine tours foregone.

c) Intercepts:

  • \(x_1 = 0 \Rightarrow x_2 = 150/25 = 6\) wine tastings (vertical intercept)
  • \(x_2 = 0 \Rightarrow x_1 = 150/30 = 5\) surf lessons (horizontal intercept)

Graph: straight line from \((0, 6)\) to \((5, 0)\).

Exercise 3: Solution ‚Äî Parts d, e, f

d) Bundle (2, 3): \(30(2) + 25(3) = 60 + 75 = 135 \leq 150\) ‚úÖ

Affordable? Yes! On the budget line? No ‚Äî she spends only ‚Ç¨135, leaving ‚Ç¨15 unspent. The bundle is inside the budget set.

e) New budget ‚Ç¨200: \(30x_1 + 25x_2 = 200 \Rightarrow x_2 = 8 - 1.2x_1\)

  • New intercepts: \((0, 8)\) and \((6.67, 0)\)
  • Parallel shift outward ‚Äî slope unchanged at \(-1.2\)

f) Price drop \(p_1 = 25\), \(M = 150\): \(25x_1 + 25x_2 = 150 \Rightarrow x_2 = 6 - x_1\)

  • New intercepts: \((0, 6)\) and \((6, 0)\)
  • Pivot outward around vertical intercept ‚Äî slope changes to \(-1\)

Key difference: (e) is a parallel shift (more of both goods equally); (f) is a pivot (relatively more surf lessons become affordable, wine tasting maximum unchanged).
